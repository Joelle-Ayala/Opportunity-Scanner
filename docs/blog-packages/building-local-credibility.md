## Core thesis

Public-sector credibility is operational evidence, not government-themed branding. A local business becomes more credible when it can show that it understands the buyer or program, is accurately registered where required, uses the right product or service categories, describes its capabilities plainly, supports claims with real performance evidence, and can follow the relevant procurement or grant process.

The best first pursuit is usually narrow enough to prove fit and capacity. It may be a small local purchase, a subcontracting role, a funded-recipient partnership, a vendor-registration step, a grant-readiness task, or a monitored opportunity. The correct path depends on the source and jurisdiction.

## ICP pain and desired outcome

### Primary pain

Local business owners often discover a grant, contract, or city initiative before they have translated their commercial track record into the information a public buyer, grant program, prime contractor, or community partner needs to evaluate them.

### Secondary pains

- The business registers in one portal but assumes that registration equals approval or qualification.
- Vendor profiles use broad marketing language instead of the category codes and capability terms procurement teams use.
- The owner cannot quickly show relevant licenses, staffing, delivery capacity, references, insurance, or prior work when a specific opportunity asks for them.
- A grant or partnership pitch is built around the business rather than the public outcome or eligibility rules.
- The team begins with a large, complex pursuit instead of a narrower path that can build legitimate experience.
- Owners are vulnerable to paid-registration or certification offers because they do not know which official registrations are free or jurisdiction-specific.

### Desired reader outcome

The reader should leave with a six-layer credibility stack and a practical first-pursuit checklist:

1. Buyer and program fit.
2. Administrative readiness.
3. Discoverability in the correct system.
4. Capability and performance proof.
5. Process and contact-path fluency.
6. A narrow, evidence-backed first pursuit.

## Audience priority

1. Local service businesses exploring city, county, school, special-district, or publicly funded demand.
2. B2B founders without a dedicated government contracting or grants team.
3. Small agencies, consultants, event producers, training companies, healthcare providers, manufacturers, suppliers, and community-service operators.
4. Economic-development and small-business advisors who need a conservative readiness framework for clients.

## The six-layer credibility stack

| Layer | What credible readiness looks like | Evidence to prepare | What it does not prove |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buyer and program fit | The offer maps to a documented public need, purchase category, program goal, or funded outcome. | Source link, relevant category codes, short fit statement, target organization, reason the timing matters. | That the organization wants to buy now. |
| Administrative readiness | The legal business information, registrations, licenses, tax information, and required representations are accurate and current for the specific path. | Official profile status, jurisdiction-required forms, licenses, insurance or bonding only when required, responsible points of contact. | That the business is pre-approved, eligible for every program, or guaranteed an award. |
| Discoverability | The business appears under the product, service, commodity, or industry categories buyers use. | Relevant NAICS or NIGP codes, capabilities narrative, keywords, service areas, contact information. | That adding many codes improves fit or replaces qualification. |
| Capability and performance proof | The business can support its claims with truthful examples of comparable work and delivery capacity. | Real references, project summaries, outcomes that can be substantiated, staffing, equipment, certifications, and performance history where applicable. | Government past performance if the business has none. Commercial work must be labeled as commercial work. |
| Process and contact-path fluency | The team knows where notices appear, how questions are submitted, which office owns the process, and whether the path is bid, grant, vendor registration, partnership, subcontracting, or monitoring. | Official portal, notice status, deadlines, source-native contact, procurement or program office, question process, partner or prime route. | Permission to bypass procurement or ethics rules through relationship building. |
| Focused first pursuit | The business chooses a plausible scope and next action that match its capacity. | Qualification checklist, pursuit owner, next action, go/no-go decision, follow-up date. | That a smaller pursuit is automatically easy or available. |

## Claim guardrails

- Do not imply that vendor registration creates approval, preferred status, eligibility, or an award.
- Do not imply that a certification is required unless the specific opportunity or program says so.
- Do not advise a business to claim public-sector past performance when it only has commercial experience.
- Do not describe a city process as universal. Name the jurisdiction and explain that portals, codes, thresholds, and rules vary.
- Do not tell readers to register everywhere. Registration should follow evidence of fit and a defined target market.
- Do not promise that a capability statement, vendor profile, networking event, or certification will produce contracts.
- Do not imply that relationship building permits off-process lobbying, gifts, undisclosed conflicts, or bypassing a competitive procurement.
- Do not generalize federal grant steps to all local grants. Every notice controls its own eligibility and application requirements.
- Do not invent percentages, award rates, buyer preferences, response times, or small-purchase thresholds.
- Do not imply that cited agencies or cities endorse Opportunity Scanner.

- Translate the offer into public-sector language without exaggerating capabilities.
- Identify whether the likely motion is sell to agency, direct apply, sell to funded buyer, sell to award recipient, partner, monitor, or research.

### H2: Complete the right registrations—and understand their limits

- Use SBA and SAM.gov to explain the federal UEI, NAICS, and SAM context.
- Use NYC as a local example of a jurisdiction-specific portal and commodity enrollment.
- Use Seattle's explicit warning that registration does not pre-approve a business.
- Tell readers to verify requirements on the exact official notice and jurisdiction page.

### H2: Make the business discoverable in buyer language

- Explain NAICS and NIGP or local commodity categories without treating them as interchangeable.
- Use Austin's official explanation that commodity codes are used to connect vendors to related solicitation notifications.
- Show how a capabilities narrative, accurate keywords, service area, and responsible contact help the right office understand fit.
- Warn against adding irrelevant codes simply to widen notifications.

### H2: Build a truthful capability proof set

Prepare a short evidence library:

1. One-sentence capability statement tied to a buyer outcome.
2. Two or three relevant commercial or public project examples, accurately labeled.
3. Verifiable references when permission exists.
4. Current licenses, certifications, insurance, bonding, equipment, staffing, or quality systems only when relevant.
5. Service geography and delivery constraints.
6. Clear primary and backup points of contact.

Use SBA's profile fields to support the structure. Do not prescribe a universal capability-statement template as a government requirement.

### H2: Learn the process before building the pitch

- Locate the official portal, current notice, deadlines, amendments, question process, and source-native contact.
- For grants, confirm eligibility before drafting; use the actual notice as controlling guidance.
- For city partnerships, identify whether the request is a procurement, sponsorship, grant, program partnership, or informal research conversation.
- Respect ethics, procurement, and communication rules. Relationship building should improve understanding, not bypass process.

### H2: Choose a focused first pursuit

Use a simple go/no-go checklist:

1. Is there an official source or documented program need?
2. Does the business have direct or adjacent evidence of capability?
3. Is the business eligible and properly registered for this path?
4. Can it meet the scope, timeline, insurance, licensing, reporting, and cash-flow requirements?
5. Is there a credible buyer, recipient, prime, partner, or program-office path?
6. What is the next action that reduces the most uncertainty?

Possible outcomes include pursue, partner, subcontract, register, clarify, monitor, or decline.
