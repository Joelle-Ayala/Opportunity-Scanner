# The 2026 Infrastructure and Construction Public-Sector Opportunity Report

**Research cutoff:** July 11, 2026

**Audience:** Contractors, specialty trades, engineering and design firms, equipment and material suppliers, construction-technology providers, environmental consultants, and infrastructure service companies

**Purpose:** A practical guide to tracing public infrastructure money into real projects, buyers, prime contracts, and subcontracting actions

## Executive brief

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA), commonly called the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (BIL), became Public Law 117-58 on November 15, 2021. Its opportunity is still active in 2026, but the actionable market is no longer captured by a headline appropriation alone. Money moves through federal agencies, formula apportionments, competitive grants, loans and capitalization grants, then through states, tribes, territories, local governments, utilities, authorities, universities, nonprofits, and private awardees. Those recipients plan projects, complete environmental and grant requirements, assemble match, advertise procurements, select primes, and issue subcontracts and purchase orders.

That chain creates several markets: direct grants, agency bids, funded-buyer sales, prime partnerships, and supplier work. The correct move depends on where the money and project are.

In April 2025, the [Government Accountability Office (GAO)](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-107243) reported that 15 agencies identified approximately $711.8 billion in IIJA appropriations available as grants to tribes, states, localities, and territories across more than 100 programs. Of that, $580.6 billion became available for obligation in FY2022-FY2025 and $131.2 billion was expected in FY2026. As of December 31, 2024, agencies had obligated $275.1 billion and outlayed $119.4 billion of the earlier available amount. These are GAO-defined grant figures, not current unspent cash or a contractor-addressable market.

The timing gap is both opportunity and warning. An appropriation gives budget authority; an apportionment or allocation assigns funds; an obligation creates a legal commitment; an outlay is a payment. A selection may precede an agreement, and an award may precede local bidding by months or years.

For 2026, the practical method is:

1. Start with a funded program, award, plan, or named project, not a broad infrastructure theme.
2. Trace the responsible federal agency and the actual recipient.
3. Determine project phase: planning, design, environmental review, right-of-way, procurement, construction, or operations.
4. Find the recipient's procurement route and likely prime contractors.
5. Qualify location, licenses, bonding, labor, domestic-content, safety, schedule, and capacity.
6. Assign the right revenue motion and a dated next action.

Opportunity Scanner joins official records into an Opportunity Signal: funding, current controller, project, fit, contact path, and next action. It should not label every award as an open bid.

## State of the market: funding is moving downstream

### 1. FY2026 is a final authorization year for many core BIL transportation amounts, not the end of project work

Programs have different availability and obligation deadlines. GAO reported that IIJA authorized and appropriated more than $551 billion to DOT for about 100 grant programs. Much surface-transportation funding covers FY2022-FY2026, but funded projects can continue later.

As of April 2025, [GAO reported](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-107166) that DOT had obligated 59% of its available IIJA grant funding and outlayed more than half of that. Awardees cited inflation, budget and schedule definition, NEPA reviews, and Build America, Buy America requirements as common challenges. In March 2026, GAO still listed its funding-status and portfolio-risk recommendations as open. Use dated records; an old headline minus obligations is not a pipeline.

### 2. Formula funding usually makes the state or local system the real market

Federal-aid highway money is apportioned to states, which program projects through state and metropolitan processes. FHWA's [FY2022-FY2026 estimates](https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/infrastructure-investment-and-jobs-act/docs/Est_FY_2022_026_Formula_Programs_Infrastructure_Investment_State-by-State_Year-by-Year.pdf) show state amounts for core highway and formula programs. They show scale, not project bids.

For project discovery, use STIPs, metropolitan TIPs, state DOT lettings, local capital plans, board agendas, and procurement portals. [FTA](https://www.transit.dot.gov/regulations-and-guidance/transportation-planning/transportation-improvement-program-tip) says a TIP includes regionally significant projects receiving FHWA or FTA funds or requiring approval. A TIP line may lack final design, permits, full funding, or a bid date, but it identifies the sponsor early.

### 3. Water funding flows through state revolving funds

EPA capitalizes Clean Water and Drinking Water State Revolving Funds; states operate financing programs and project lists. Vendors should normally track the state program and local utility, not pitch EPA as the project buyer.

EPA issued [FY2026 SRF allotments](https://www.epa.gov/dwsrf/fiscal-year-2026-allotments-state-revolving-fund-provisions-infrastructure-investment-and) in April 2026; its memorandum keeps a 20% state match for FY2026 IIJA General Supplemental capitalization grants. EPA's May 2026 lead-service-line allotments give eligible jurisdictions until September 30, 2027 to apply for and receive those funds. Allotment is not a construction notice; follow the state's project list and the utility's schedule.

### 4. Broadband is transitioning from state selection into implementation

The $42.45 billion BEAD program runs through states and territories. In March 2026, [BroadbandUSA reported](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/news/latest-news/broadbandusa-monthly-newsletter-march-2026) 53 approved final proposals, 50 with NIST approval making funds available, and 38 signed award agreements. This is a March snapshot, not July status.

Useful records are the [state BEAD plans, portals, and milestones](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/funding-programs/broadband-equity-access-and-deployment-bead-program/public-resources-related-bead-plans-and-milestones), subgrantee selections, agreements, permits, and provider build plans. Vendors normally sell to the selected subgrantee or network prime unless state procurement says otherwise.

### 5. Energy and grid opportunities mix public and private recipients

DOE can fund states, tribes, utilities, authorities, manufacturers, and consortia. Its [GRIP project page](https://www.energy.gov/oe/grid-resilience-and-innovation-partnerships-grip-program-projects) lists parties, cost shares, status, round, and location, but says content reflects December 2024 information. "Selected" and "awarded" are not interchangeable, and neither proves an open subcontract.

DOE's February 2026 [$1.9 billion SPARK opportunity](https://www.energy.gov/articles/energy-department-announces-19b-investment-critical-grid-infrastructure-reduce-electricity) had concept papers due April 2, applications due May 20, and selections anticipated in August. As of July 11, monitor selections and recipient supply chains; applications are closed.

### 6. Direct federal construction remains a separate lane

Some work is procured directly, including USACE civil works. USACE publishes work plans and district forecasts; SAM.gov remains the governmentwide contract-opportunity entry point. [USACE guidance](https://www.usace.army.mil/Strategic-Partnerships/Small-Business/Upcoming-Contract-Opportunities/) recommends tracking planned and solicited work and using district small-business offices. A work-plan line is not a solicitation.

## Eight opportunity lanes

| Lane | Buyer or counterparty | Typical source signal | Revenue motion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Direct federal construction | USACE, GSA, federal land or facility agency | Work plan, forecast, SAM notice | Direct prime or Sell to Award Recipient |
| State formula project | State DOT or state agency | Apportionment, STIP, letting schedule | Sell to Agency or prime |
| Local/MPO transportation project | City, county, transit authority, port, airport | TIP, capital plan, board action, local RFP | Sell to Funded Buyer |
| Water and wastewater project | State SRF recipient, utility, municipality | Intended-use plan, project priority list, bond/board action | Sell to Funded Buyer |
| Broadband deployment | State/territory subgrantee or selected provider | Approved final proposal, subgrant, provider announcement | Sell to Award Recipient |
| Grid and energy project | Utility, state, tribe, authority, consortium | DOE selection/award, commission filing, recipient procurement | Sell to Award Recipient or Partner with Recipient |
| Materials and equipment | Prime, recipient, distributor | Bid tab, specifications, procurement package | Channel / Distributor Motion |
| Planning, permitting, compliance, and operations | Sponsor, engineer, construction manager, prime | Planning study, grant agreement, environmental record | Sell to Funded Buyer or Award Recipient |

Direct Apply is appropriate only where the company is an eligible applicant and wants to carry the grant's administrative, match, reporting, and performance obligations. Most private construction vendors will more often sell to the agency, funded recipient, or prime.

## How to trace funding into a project

### Step 1: Name the federal program and money type

Record the Assistance Listing or program name, federal agency and subagency, statutory source, fiscal year, formula versus discretionary structure, award instrument, and period of availability. Label amounts accurately: authorized, appropriated, apportioned, allocated, selected, obligated, outlayed, award ceiling, or project total. Include nonfederal match separately.

If the source says "up to" or "selected for negotiation," preserve that language. Do not convert it to an award.

### Step 2: Identify who controls the next decision

The federal agency may have already transferred the practical buying decision. Name the prime recipient, subrecipient if reported, project sponsor, implementing department, procurement office, and governing board. For formula transportation, this may be the state DOT or local sponsor. For SRF, it is often a state financing authority followed by a utility. For BEAD, it is the state broadband office followed by the selected subgrantee. For a DOE award, it may be a utility or consortium lead.

### Step 3: Locate the project record

Search for a project title, location, route, facility, utility system, award identifier, and recipient. Useful official records include STIPs/TIPs, intended-use plans, environmental review pages, grant agreements, capital plans, council or authority agendas, engineering solicitations, plan-holder lists, pre-bid meetings, bid tabs, award recommendations, and notices to proceed.

A project title can change between the federal award and local procurement. Preserve aliases and identifiers in Opportunity Scanner.

### Step 4: Determine phase and procurement window

Use these phases:

- **Programmed:** appears in a plan or funding list; scope and timing may move.
- **Selected:** announced for funding but award conditions may remain.
- **Awarded/obligated:** legal commitment exists; recipient procurement may still be pending.
- **Planning/environmental:** consultants and technical services may be actionable before construction.
- **Design/right-of-way/permitting:** specifications and bid packaging are forming.
- **Advertised:** procurement is open; amendments and pre-bid requirements control.
- **Awarded to prime:** subcontract, supplier, staffing, and execution routes may remain.
- **Construction/operations:** change orders, later phases, maintenance, inspection, and replacement may remain, subject to procurement rules.

### Step 5: Map the contract chain

Draw the chain: federal agency -> recipient -> sponsor -> design/construction manager -> prime -> subcontractor/supplier. USAspending reports federal prime and subaward data; state and local records may sit in recipient systems, business directories, or prime notices.

### Step 6: Build a contact path

Use role-based routes: notice contact, small-business office, program manager, sponsor procurement office, engineer, plan room, prime estimator, pre-bid conference, or public meeting. Respect post-advertisement communication rules. The correct path may be registration, a formal question, or a subcontractor portal.

## Qualification framework

Score 0, 1, or 2 for each factor:

| Factor | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scope fit | General adjacency | Partial package fit | Exact trade/product/service fit |
| Funding evidence | Headline only | Program/project listed | Award/plan and amount type verified |
| Phase/timing | Too late or unknown | Future with milestone | Actionable pre-bid, bid, or prime-buy window |
| Buyer/chain clarity | Unknown | Recipient known | Sponsor, procurement route, and prime known |
| Geography/capacity | Outside reach | Capacity requires partner | Can staff, mobilize, and deliver |
| Compliance readiness | Major unknowns | Gaps with owner | Licenses, safety, labor, domestic content understood |
| Financial readiness | Cannot support | Bonding/cash flow to confirm | Bonding, insurance, cash flow and terms workable |
| Competitive position | No proof/access | Transferable proof | Relevant proof, relationship, certification, or package advantage |
| Next action | Generic outreach | Contact hypothesis | Named owner, channel, deliverable, and date |

**14-18: High Actionability.** Begin capture, bid, or prime outreach.

**9-13: Medium Actionability.** Resolve phase, route, or compliance gaps first.

**0-8: Low Actionability.** Monitor or archive.

Apply hard gates separately: bidder eligibility, license, bonding, mandatory pre-bid attendance, geographic restriction, certification, security or badging, approved-equals rule, Buy America/Build America Buy America (BABA), Davis-Bacon, disadvantaged-business requirements, insurance, schedule, and bid deadline. Requirements vary by program and contract; do not infer them from the BIL label alone.

## Compliance factors that change the pursuit

The Build America, Buy America Act establishes domestic-preference requirements for iron, steel, manufactured products, and construction materials used in covered infrastructure projects under federal financial assistance. The [Made in America Office](https://www.madeinamerica.gov/resources/financial-assistance/) publishes agency waiver information and explains that proposed waivers generally receive a 15-day public review. Applicability, product standards, and waivers are award-specific. Suppliers should document origin, manufacturing process, component data where required, and any approved waiver rather than offer a generic "Made in USA" statement.

The [Department of Labor's BIL fact sheet](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/fact-sheets/66a) says a vast majority of BIL-authorized construction funding requires Davis-Bacon prevailing wages through direct coverage or a Related Act. Covered recipients must insert applicable clauses and wage determinations; contractors and subcontractors have pay and recordkeeping duties. Broadband programs under Division F are treated differently in that fact sheet, illustrating why the contract and funding statute must be checked. As of July 2026, DOL also notes litigation affecting portions of its 2023 Davis-Bacon rule on its [current construction guidance page](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/government-contracts/construction); obtain current counsel or agency guidance for disputed provisions.

Other recurring gates include NEPA and permitting, disadvantaged business enterprise or local participation goals, certified payroll, Buy America regimes specific to transportation, OSHA and owner safety rules, performance and payment bonds, cyber requirements for connected assets, and grant flow-down clauses. Price compliance and reporting work into the job.

## A 30-day operating plan

### Days 1-5: Define capacity and territory

Document trades and NAICS codes, licenses, bonding range, insurance, crew and equipment capacity, mobilization radius, union/nonunion constraints, certifications, safety metrics, past projects, material origins, and ideal package size. Separate prime-ready work from subcontract-ready work. Choose no more than three infrastructure lanes and five target geographies.

### Days 6-10: Build a 50-record funding-to-project set

Use Opportunity Scanner to collect federal program records, dated awards, recipients, project plans, and procurement sources. At least half the records should be downstream of the federal agency: STIP/TIP projects, SRF lists, recipient board actions, BEAD subgrantees, utility filings, local advertisements, USACE district forecasts, or prime notices. Store official URL, date, amount type, project phase, location, recipient, and identifier.

### Days 11-15: Trace and score

Deduplicate projects appearing across several sources. For the top 20, draw the contract chain and find the recipient procurement portal. Score each and apply hard gates. Select no more than 12 pursuits: a mix of pre-bid positioning, advertised procurements, prime/subcontract routes, and monitored projects. Closed grants can remain only when they reveal a funded recipient with future procurement.

### Days 16-21: Establish source-native contact

Register in required vendor and plan-room systems. Attend available pre-bid or industry events. Ask formal questions through the stated channel. For pre-procurement projects, give the sponsor or engineer a short capability statement tied to location, package, schedule, and proof. For awarded projects, approach the prime's estimator or supplier team with a specific scope and capacity. Log every contact and follow-up date.

### Days 22-26: Prepare bid readiness

Confirm bonding, insurance, labor rates, wage determination, material origin, lead times, approved-equal process, permits, safety submittals, quality plan, schedule, cash-flow exposure, retainage, payment terms, and flow-downs. Line up partners for gaps. Review addenda and acknowledge them. Set a no-bid threshold for margin, risk, geography, and capacity.

### Days 27-30: Hold a pipeline review

Advance each record to pursue, partner, monitor, or stop. Every active pursuit should show: controlling source, current phase, buyer/prime, amount type, fit, compliance gates, decision date, owner, and next action. Track leading indicators such as plan-room access, estimator conversations, approved vendor status, prequalification, and invitation to quote, not just bids submitted.

## Caveats and responsible use

- This report is not legal, labor, environmental, grant, engineering, or bonding advice. The award and contract control.
- IIJA/BIL, IRA, annual appropriations, state funds, bonds, and ratepayer funds may coexist in one project. Do not attribute the full project value to BIL without evidence.
- "Selected," "awarded," "obligated," and "outlayed" are different statuses. Preserve the source's term.
- Formula estimates and grant allocations are not bid values. Project totals may include match, financing, design, right-of-way, contingency, and nonconstruction costs.
- A federal award to a state or utility does not make every downstream purchase a federal procurement; recipient rules and federal flow-downs both matter.
- Program policy, waiver status, grant agreements, schedules, and administration priorities can change. Re-verify before outreach or bid.
- Use role-based public contacts and permitted procurement channels. Do not seek nonpublic bid information.
