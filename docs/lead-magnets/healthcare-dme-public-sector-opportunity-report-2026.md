# The 2026 Healthcare and DME Public-Sector Opportunity Report

Subtitle: A practical guide to VA procurement, Medicare supplier signals, funded providers, recipients, channel partners, and source-backed next actions.

Status: Complete manuscript; ready for designed PDF and gated web delivery.

As-of date: July 10, 2026. Time-sensitive Medicare enrollment rules must be rechecked before publication and on every update.

## Offer and audience

This report is for healthcare suppliers, DMEPOS companies, medical-supply manufacturers and distributors, rehabilitation and mobility businesses, healthcare technology companies, and revenue teams evaluating public-sector growth.

Conversion promise: Understand the distinct public-sector opportunity paths in healthcare and use a repeatable method to qualify them. The report does not determine Medicare eligibility, accreditation, coding, reimbursement, contracting eligibility, clinical compliance, or revenue potential.

## Executive brief

Healthcare public-sector opportunity is not one market. It includes agency procurement, Federal Supply Schedule routes, Medicare supplier and reimbursement requirements, publicly funded health centers and providers, grant recipients, distributors, prime vendors, and policy signals. Each lane uses different evidence and demands a different action.

National health spending provides context for the scale and policy importance of healthcare, but it is not the addressable market for a DME or medical-supply company. CMS projects national health expenditure growth and publishes Medicare data, fee schedules, supplier guidance, and policy changes. VA publishes medical equipment and supply schedule information. HRSA publishes current and historical grant and health-center data. SAM.gov and USAspending provide procurement and federal award evidence.

The highest-value work is separating these lanes before outreach. A reimbursement rule may require Monitor Policy or supplier-readiness work. A VA schedule may support a contracting pathway. A HRSA recipient may support funded-buyer or partner research. A prior award may reveal an incumbent, distributor, or agency pattern without proving current demand.

## State of the market and opportunity signals

### Trend 1: Healthcare spending and public funding remain large, but broad totals require restraint

CMS's current National Health Expenditure fact sheet projects average NHE growth of 5.4 percent from 2025 through 2034, with Medicare spending projected to grow 7.7 percent per year on average. HRSA reports more than 5,500 grant awards annually and reports over $11.1 billion in FY2025 grants across primary care, health workforce, HIV/AIDS, maternal and child health, rural health, and other programs.

Business implication: Public money affects many healthcare organizations and delivery systems, but those totals combine categories that are not interchangeable and do not measure DME demand.

Recommended action: Use program, recipient, service, product, geography, and time filters to find the narrow records that map to the offer.

### Trend 2: Medicare supplier participation is a readiness and compliance lane, not a generic sales lead

CMS states that DMEPOS suppliers seeking Medicare reimbursement generally must obtain accreditation from a CMS-approved organization, enroll in Medicare, and post a $50,000 surety bond for each NPI they maintain, subject to stated exemptions and program details.

Business implication: A reimbursement or supplier record may identify market structure, competitors, referring patterns, or compliance work. It does not automatically create a buyer or open opportunity.

Recommended action: Route Medicare signals to supplier-readiness, reimbursement, compliance, product-category, or provider-market research before deciding whether outreach is appropriate.

### Trend 3: A temporary 2026 enrollment moratorium changes the direct supplier path

CMS reports that on February 27, 2026 it implemented a temporary nationwide moratorium on initial Medicare enrollment for specified DMEPOS medical supply company categories. CMS states the moratorium lasts six months and may be extended in six-month increments.

Business implication: A company affected by the moratorium should not interpret supplier-enrollment content as an immediately available path. The status is time-sensitive and does not eliminate separate procurement, distribution, manufacturer, technology, service, funded-provider, or partner opportunities.

Recommended action: Verify the current CMS moratorium page and definitions before taking enrollment action. Consult the appropriate enrollment contractor or qualified advisor for company-specific questions.

### Trend 4: VA procurement includes category-specific schedule routes

VA's Schedule 65 II A covers medical equipment and supplies across categories such as surgical dressings, instruments, hospital equipment, clothing, ophthalmic equipment, kits, and medical equipment maintenance. VA identifies SAM.gov as the official source for government contract opportunities while publishing schedule solicitation materials and ordering information.

Business implication: Product category, Federal Supply Code, NAICS, schedule requirements, and ordering route matter. A company may also participate through a distributor, schedule holder, prime, or subcontracting relationship.

Recommended action: Map products to the applicable VA schedule and codes, inspect active notices separately, and compare direct schedule, agency procurement, distributor, and partner motions.

### Trend 5: Funded providers and recipients create a distinct buyer and partner lane

HRSA publishes active and awarded grants, health-center awardee data, and service-delivery sites. HRSA notes that FY2026 award data is preliminary and current-year-to-date, and defines an active grant by project-period end date rather than proof of new current-year funding.

Business implication: A funded health center or recipient may be a buyer, partner, implementation site, or research target, but the funding purpose and active project scope must fit the offer.

Recommended action: Verify program purpose, project period, recipient role, and likely purchasing authority before creating outreach.

## Six healthcare opportunity lanes

| Lane | Evidence | Typical revenue motion | First validation |
|---|---|---|---|
| VA and agency procurement | Schedule pages, SAM.gov notices, award history, vendor portals | Sell to Agency; Channel / Distributor Motion | Product code, notice status, contract vehicle, buyer route |
| Medicare supplier and reimbursement | CMS enrollment, accreditation, fee schedule, policy, claims data | Monitor Policy; Research Only; supplier-readiness | Current rule, eligibility, code, geography, data period |
| Funded providers and health centers | HRSA grants, USAspending, recipient and site data | Sell to Funded Buyer; Partner with Recipient | Program purpose, project period, purchasing role |
| Prime, distributor, and manufacturer channels | Award records, VA catalogs, SBA subcontracting, recipient sites | Sell to Award Recipient; Channel / Distributor Motion | Existing vehicle, category coverage, partner fit |
| Direct grants and assistance | Grants.gov and agency program notices | Direct Apply; Partner with Recipient | Eligibility, status, deadline, allowable use |
| Policy and emerging demand | Federal Register, CMS rules, agency guidance | Monitor Policy; Research Only | Effective date, final versus proposed rule, business impact |

## The healthcare opportunity workflow

### Step 1: Build a product and readiness profile

Record products, HCPCS or product categories when known, clinical and non-clinical use cases, customers, delivery geography, licenses, certifications, accreditation, reimbursement status, manufacturing or distributor relationships, and implementation capacity. Unknown fields should become validation tasks, not assumptions.

### Step 2: Translate into public-sector lanes

Map each offer to the six lanes above. A mobility product may map to VA patient mobility schedules, rehabilitation programs, DMEPOS policy, funded providers, and distributor partners. A healthcare software product may map to health-center recipients, agency modernization, prime implementers, and program operations without being a DMEPOS supplier.

### Step 3: Search the source that matches the question

Use VA schedule and procurement pages for category and vehicle research; SAM.gov for active federal notices; USAspending for award and recipient patterns; CMS for reimbursement, supplier, fee schedule, and claims context; HRSA for recipients, active grants, and health-center sites; Grants.gov for open assistance; Federal Register for policy changes.

### Step 4: Verify status and constraint

Check notice type, deadline, amendment, project period, data year, proposed versus final rule, enrollment restriction, and the definition behind every metric. Recheck the 2026 DMEPOS moratorium immediately before publishing or advising action.

### Step 5: Choose target and revenue motion

Name the agency, provider, recipient, prime, distributor, manufacturer, program office, or policy owner. Then select one motion. Do not turn a CMS data record into a sales lead unless a separate buyer path exists.

### Step 6: Build a compliant contact path and next action

Prefer procurement contacts, schedule support, program offices, official vendor registration, recipient leadership, prime or distributor business-development roles, and documented source contacts. The next action should validate category fit, active need, purchasing authority, or partner role.

## Worked illustrative example

Company context: A manufacturer sells rehabilitation supports and related clinical supplies through distributors.

Evidence: VA Schedule 65 II A includes relevant medical and surgical supply categories; an historical federal award shows an incumbent supplier serving a related category; a HRSA-funded provider operates rehabilitation services in the target region.

Interpretation: The schedule is a contracting route, the award is market evidence, and the funded provider is a possible buyer or partner. None proves an active purchase.

Primary motion: Channel / Distributor Motion through a qualified schedule holder or incumbent supplier.

Secondary research: Validate whether the funded provider buys the category and whether a separate local procurement route exists.

Next action: Map exact product codes and schedule requirements, then identify the distributor or contract owner responsible for the category. This is illustrative, not a customer result.

## 30-day action plan

Week 1: Complete product/readiness profile and select two priority lanes.

Week 2: Build a source-backed list of 10 agencies, recipients, primes, distributors, or providers. Label each record active, historical, policy, route, or research only.

Week 3: Qualify the top five by category fit, geography, current status, purchasing path, and compliance constraints.

Week 4: Create owned next actions for the strongest rows and place the rest in monitor or rejected states. Recheck any time-sensitive CMS or solicitation status.

## Limits and warnings

- This report is not legal, clinical, coding, accreditation, reimbursement, procurement, or eligibility advice.
- CMS policies, moratoria, fee schedules, and enrollment requirements can change. Verify current official guidance.
- National health spending and HRSA totals are context, not DME market size or sales potential.
- Medicare claims datasets can exclude Medicare Advantage and other populations, suppress small counts, and lag the current year.
- A supplier directory entry is not proof of active operations, purchasing intent, or product fit.
- A grant recipient is not automatically a buyer.
- VA schedule coverage does not guarantee an award or order.
- Historical awards reveal patterns but are not open opportunities.
