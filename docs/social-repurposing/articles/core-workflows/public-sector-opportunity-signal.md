## Audience

Founders, revenue and partnerships teams, analysts, and operators who need a shared definition for turning public procurement, funding, award, forecast, policy, workforce, reimbursement, and money-flow records into qualified business-development work.

## Core Thesis

A public-sector opportunity signal is a sourced external record indicating a possible opportunity. “Possible” is essential: a signal is not automatically a lead, open bid, grant, or qualified deal. It earns action only after evidence class, status, target, fit, revenue motion, contact path, next action, and disqualifier are clear.

## Five Reusable Angles

1. **Signal is not lead:** A sourced record becomes useful only after interpretation and qualification.
2. **Six evidence classes:** Active, historical, forecasted, funded-recipient, policy, and research evidence need distinct labels.
3. **Evidence before relevance:** A record can look relevant while its status or source does not support the proposed action.
4. **Disqualifiers create trust:** Weak fit, failed eligibility, stale timing, unrealistic capacity, or no route should stop promotion.
5. **The final unit is owned work:** The action row connects source truth to one decision and one owner.

## Verified Proof And Caveat Notes

- **Verified:** SAM.gov notice type and response instructions determine whether a record is a solicitation, planning signal, award notice, or other evidence.
- **Verified:** FAR 15.201 says an RFI may be used for planning when the government does not presently intend to award a contract; responses are not offers.
- **Verified:** USAspending defines a recipient as an entity or person receiving federal funding.
- **Caveat:** Recipient evidence proves money flow to an organization, not a specific downstream need or current buying intent.
- **Verified:** Grants.gov distinguishes forecasted, posted, closed, and archived opportunities; forecasted records are not guaranteed to post.
- **Verified:** FederalRegister.gov provides official policy records, but a proposed or final rule is not itself a procurement.
- **Status rule:** Never convert “possible” into “available,” “qualified,” “funded buyer,” or “open opportunity” without the corresponding source support.

## LinkedIn Posts

### 1. Founder POV

We use the phrase “opportunity signal” very deliberately.

Not lead. Not deal. Not open contract.

A public-sector opportunity signal is a sourced external record that may indicate procurement, funding, partnership, policy, workforce, reimbursement, or money-flow activity relevant to a company.

The word “may” does real work.

A Sources Sought notice can show market research, not an award path. A historical award can reveal a buyer or recipient, not open budget. A grant forecast can point to future funding, not a guaranteed announcement. A policy record can indicate emerging demand, not a purchase.

The signal earns promotion only when we can answer: What does the source prove? What is its status? Who is the target? Which revenue motion fits? What is the credible contact path? What action reduces uncertainty? What would disqualify it?

That discipline makes the output smaller. Good. A short list of traceable, owned decisions is more useful than a large list of records dressed up as pipeline.

### 2. Practical Framework

Use six classes to keep public-sector signals honest:

1. Active evidence: a current notice or posting with an action controlled by the source.
2. Historical evidence: prior awards, recipients, incumbents, and buying patterns.
3. Forecasted evidence: planned procurement or funding that may change or never post.
4. Funded-recipient evidence: verified money flow to an organization, with downstream need still to validate.
5. Policy evidence: rules, budgets, plans, or notices that may shape future demand.
6. Research evidence: useful context that does not yet support a pursuit.

Then run the disqualifier test. Is the match weak? Is timing stale? Does eligibility fail? Is delivery unrealistic? Is the target unsupported? Is there no lawful, credible route?

Only retained signals become action rows: official source, status, target, primary revenue motion, contact path, next action, owner, and disqualifier.

Classification prevents a research clue from masquerading as an active deal.

### 3. Source-Backed Insight

An RFI can look like a sales opportunity. FAR 15.201 provides the needed boundary.

The regulation allows agencies to use RFIs for planning when the government does not presently intend to award a contract. Responses are not offers and cannot be accepted to form a binding contract.

That does not make an RFI unimportant. It may be a strong early signal. A relevant company could review the instructions, decide whether a capability response is worthwhile, learn how the agency frames the need, and monitor for later procurement activity.

But the label controls the action. Calling it an “open contract” would manufacture urgency and misstate the source.

The same rule applies elsewhere: a USAspending recipient is not automatically a buyer; a Grants.gov forecast is not a posted opportunity; a Federal Register document is not a purchase.

Public-sector intelligence gets more useful when the evidence class stays attached to every recommendation.
