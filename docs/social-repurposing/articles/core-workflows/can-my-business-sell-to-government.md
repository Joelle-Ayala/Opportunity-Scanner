## Audience

Founders and commercial operators who see possible public-sector demand but need to decide whether to pursue a direct agency sale, prepare for later, subcontract, sell to a funded organization, partner, monitor, research, or stop.

## Core Thesis

The useful question is not simply whether a business can sell to government. It is which route fits now. Readiness requires evidence of demand, operational capacity, attainable administrative requirements, and a credible route to the buyer or partner.

## Five Reusable Angles

1. **Four readiness gates:** Market, performance, administrative, and route gates create a practical go/no-go assessment.
2. **Registration is not demand:** SAM.gov registration supports direct federal awards but does not make a company competitive.
3. **Small is procurement-specific:** Small-business status depends on the NAICS code and current SBA size standard for the procurement.
4. **Prime is not the only route:** Subcontracting, funded-buyer, recipient-partner, and research motions can fit a capable commercial company.
5. **No-bid can be correct:** Proposal effort, compliance, cash flow, timing, and delivery burden belong in the decision.

## Verified Proof And Caveat Notes

- **Verified:** SBA recommends first testing whether government buys what a company sells, the size of that market, and which agencies buy it.
- **Verified:** SAM.gov is free; active registration enables an entity to bid on federal contracts or apply for federal assistance.
- **Caveat:** Registration takes time, requires maintenance, and does not prove demand, competitiveness, eligibility for a specific notice, or likely success.
- **Verified:** SBA size standards vary by NAICS code and commonly use receipts or employee counts; affiliation rules may affect the calculation.
- **Verified:** SBA distinguishes prime contractors from subcontractors working for primes.
- **Verified:** APEX Accelerators can assist with readiness, registration, certifications, and past-opportunity research.
- **Status rule:** Historical awards and forecasts can validate a market, but neither is an open solicitation.

## LinkedIn Posts

### 1. Founder POV

“Can my business sell to the government?” sounds like a yes-or-no question.

It usually is not.

The real decision is which route fits your company today.

Maybe you have the demand evidence, delivery capacity, cash flow, and administrative readiness to pursue a direct agency purchase. Maybe your specialty fits better behind an established prime. Maybe a funded organization is the more credible target. Maybe the evidence supports preparation or monitoring, not outreach.

I would rather see a company make that decision explicitly than treat registration as progress by itself.

Use four gates:

Market: do official records show relevant, repeated demand?
Performance: can you deliver the likely scope and timing?
Administrative: are the classification, registration, documentation, and compliance requirements attainable?
Route: can you name the office, portal, prime, recipient, or adviser that makes the next step concrete?

The outcome can be pursue, prepare, partner, subcontract, monitor, research, or stop. “Not prime-ready” is not the same as “no public-sector path.”

### 2. Practical Framework

Before pursuing a government opportunity, run this four-gate check:

1. Market gate
Look for repeated official evidence that public buyers purchase what you already sell. One broad keyword match is not a market.

2. Performance gate
Check staffing, delivery geography, schedule, insurance, security or privacy requirements, quality controls, invoicing, and cash flow.

3. Administrative gate
Confirm the relevant NAICS code, current SBA size standard, registration needs, representations, and required documentation.

4. Route gate
Name the agency contact, response process, vendor portal, prime contractor, funded-recipient role, or qualified adviser connected to the next step.

Pass all four before treating a direct pursuit as active. If market and performance pass but administration does not, create a readiness plan. If prime delivery is unrealistic, test a subcontracting or partner route. If the market gate repeatedly fails, stop collecting portals.

Readiness is a decision system, not a registration checklist.

### 3. Source-Backed Insight

SAM.gov registration is free, and an active registration enables an entity to bid on federal contracts or apply for federal financial assistance.

Useful fact. Incomplete strategy.

Registration does not tell you whether an agency buys what you sell, whether your company can meet the terms, whether the pursuit fits your cash flow, or whether you are competitive for a specific requirement.

SBA’s guidance starts earlier: assess whether government buys your product or service, how large that market appears to be, and which agencies buy it. Then check operational readiness and the requirements attached to the actual opportunity.

Small-business status also is not one permanent label. For a federal procurement, a company must meet the size standard tied to the assigned NAICS code, and affiliation rules can affect the calculation.

Treat registration as one administrative gate inside a larger readiness decision. Market evidence, performance capacity, and a credible route still have to pass.
